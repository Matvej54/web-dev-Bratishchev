Paragraphs

Selector specificity in CSS determines which styles take precedence when multiple rules target the same element. It's calculated based on the components of the selector: inline styles, IDs, classes/attributes/pseudo-classes, and element types/pseudo-elements. The specificity is represented as "a, b, c, d," where higher values indicate higher specificity. For example, an ID selector has a higher specificity than a class selector.

Complex selectors in CSS combine multiple simple selectors to target specific elements based on their relationship with other elements in the HTML structure. They include descendant selectors (` `), child selectors (`>`), adjacent sibling selectors (`+`), and general sibling selectors (`~`). These selectors provide a flexible way to apply styles to elements based on their position and relationship within the document.

Pseudo-classes target elements based on their state or user interaction, like :hover or :active. Pseudo-elements target specific parts of elements, such as ::first-line or ::before, or generate content before or after an element's content box.
